Myanmar Cuisine

Mohinga

  • Description: Myanmar's national dish, a fish soup with rice noodles. It's usually eaten for breakfast but is popular all day. The soup is made from fish and flavored with lemongrass and ginger.
  • Common Add-ons: You can add fried fritters, boiled eggs, and lime.

For a detailed explanation of this dish, you can read more about it on Burmalicious.

Shan Noodles

  • Description: A noodle dish from Shan State with thin rice noodles in a light broth or served dry. It’s topped with chicken or pork and vegetables like tomatoes.
  • Common Add-ons:Add garlic oil, peanuts, or chili flakes for more flavor.

Laphet Thoke (Tea Leaf Salad)

  • Description: A special salad made from fermented tea leaves, mixed with crunchy peanuts, garlic, and sesame seeds. It has a strong, unique flavor.
  • Common Add-ons: Sometimes includes tomatoes, cabbage, and a squeeze of lime.

Ohn No Khao Swe (Coconut Noodle Soup)

  • Description: A creamy noodle soup made with chicken and coconut milk. It’s like a mild curry with wheat noodles, often topped with onions and cilantro.
  • Common Add-ons: Hard-boiled eggs, lime, and chili powder can be added.

Burmese Curry (Hin)

  • Description: A mild curry made with chicken, beef, or fish, served with rice and several small side dishes.
  • Common Add-ons: Sometimes comes with pickles, soup, or fresh vegetables.

Ngapi Yay (Fermented Fish Paste Dip)

  • What it is: Ngapi Yay is a salty dip made from fermented fish paste. It has a strong flavor and is a staple in many Myanmar meals, especially in rural areas. People love eating it with a variety of fresh and boiled vegetables like cucumbers, carrots, and leafy greens. The dip can be spicy or mild, depending on personal taste.
  • Why it's special: It’s one of the most traditional dishes in Myanmar, showing the country’s love for simple, yet bold, flavors.

Kyay Oh (Noodle Soup)

  • Description: Kyay Oh is a clear noodle soup made with rice or egg noodles, usually served with pork or chicken, green vegetables, and quail eggs. The soup is light but flavorful, often spiced with garlic and ginger. There’s also a dry version where the noodles and toppings are served without broth.
  • Why it's special: Kyay Oh is loved by many in Myanmar because it’s filling, healthy, and can be enjoyed as a main meal any time of the day. The clear broth makes it a lighter option compared to richer soups like Mohinga.

Mont Lin Ma Yar

  • What is it: These little rice flour snacks are crispy on the outside and soft on the inside. They’re called “Husband and Wife” because two halves are stuck together like a couple. They are often filled with quail eggs, beans, or chopped green onions, making them both savory and slightly sweet.
  • Why it's special: Mont Lin Ma Yar is a fun and delicious street food, popular with both kids and adults. It’s commonly found at markets or street stalls and is a great snack to share with friends or family.

Mont Lone Yay Paw

  • Description: Mont Lone Yay Paw are sweet, chewy rice flour balls filled with palm sugar. These little treats are boiled until they float to the surface of the water, which is where the name comes from—"Lone Yay Paw" means "floating on water." Once cooked, they are rolled in freshly grated coconut for extra texture and sweetness.
  • Why it's special:This dessert is commonly made during the Thingyan Water Festival, Myanmar's New Year celebration, and is shared among friends and family as a symbol of joy and togetherness. Eating Mont Lone Yay Paw is also a fun experience because sometimes, a few balls are mischievously filled with salt instead of sugar, leading to laughter when someone bites into one!

Thingyan Htamin (Thingyan Rice)

  • Description: Thingyan Htamin is a special rice dish made during the Thingyan Water Festival, which celebrates the Myanmar New Year. It’s a simple, yet flavorful rice mixed with sesame seeds, fried shallots, and sometimes coconut shavings. The rice is lightly seasoned with salt and oil to enhance its taste.
  • Why it's special: This dish is traditionally made for sharing during the festival. It’s often given as an offering to monks and distributed to friends and neighbors as a gesture of goodwill. The simplicity of Thingyan Htamin reflects the joy and warmth of the festival, bringing people together to celebrate.
